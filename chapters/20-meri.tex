\chapter{Meri}

Meri is at the transit desk when I arrive for my shift.

She is filling in a form. Her own form, for her own case. Under mode of concealment, she has circled hat. I do not know when she started doing this. I have not asked about her cases. She has not asked about mine since the break room conversation two weeks ago. We have, by mutual non-agreement, stopped asking each other questions that produce uncomfortable answers.

``Tert,'' she says, not looking up from the form.

``Meri.''

``Your subject. The widower.'' She finishes writing something and looks up. ``You like him.''

This is not a question.

``That is not the word,'' I say.

``I know,'' she says.

She goes back to her form. I fill in mine. I have three active cases. Tonight's other option is a sophomore in a garden-level apartment with drop ceilings. There is nothing I can do with a drop ceiling. I write forty-seven-C on the destination line. I circle hat. I open the door.
