# Countermeasures

**Status:** EXPLORATORY (2026-04-11)

The human tradition of anti-demonic countermeasures, as experienced by a field operative. These are occupational nuisances, not existential threats. Tert's attitude toward them ranges from professional irritation to complete indifference to grudging respect, depending on whether they actually interfere with the work.

The comedy register: Tert talks about countermeasures the way a pest control technician talks about home remedies. Some are harmless nonsense. Some are mildly inconvenient. A few actually work, for reasons nobody can explain, which mirrors the theological ambiguity (the whole system operates on mechanisms nobody has validated).

---

## Salt

Professional assessment: irrelevant. Salt is sodium chloride. It sits on surfaces. It does not interact with atmospheric operations in any measurable way. Humans have been lining doorways with it for centuries. The only documented effect is that it makes the floor gritty and occasionally gets into the subject's shoes, which is more of an annoyance to the subject than to the operative.

The Operations Manual has a section on salt. It is four paragraphs long and the conclusion is "exercise standard caution." Nobody has ever been able to articulate what standard caution with respect to salt looks like in practice.

Tert's personal view: fine on eggs.

## Holy Water

Professional assessment: ambiguous. Sometimes produces a localized thermal reaction on contact with an operative. Sometimes does not. There is no consensus on why. Controlled studies have been proposed and rejected by Compliance on the grounds that the experimental design would require operatives to voluntarily make contact with a blessed substance, which raises HR concerns.

The prevailing theory in the field is that efficacy correlates with the sincerity of the blessing, but sincerity is not a measurable variable, which puts this in the same epistemological category as the Fear Index.

Irrelevant to Gordon's case. Gordon does not own holy water. Gordon owns tap water and a coffee maker.

## Crucifixes and Religious Iconography

Professional assessment: mildly irritating at close range, in the way that a bright light is irritating without being dangerous. The mechanism is unknown. Newer iconography tends to be less effective than older pieces, which has generated an internal theory that the objects accumulate something over time, though what that something is has not been determined.

Gordon does not have religious iconography in the house. There is a framed print in the hallway that is too small to make out. It is not religious. It appears to be a watercolor of a bridge.

## Sage / Smudging

Professional assessment: no measurable effect on operations. The smoke is unpleasant in the same way all smoke is unpleasant. Humans who burn sage tend to also open windows, which affects air currents, which can interfere with thermal pocket work. The interference is from the open window, not the sage.

## Prayers

Professional assessment: variable and unexplained. A person praying in a room changes the room's atmospheric signature in a way that some operatives can detect and others cannot. Whether this is a real phenomenon or a reporting bias has been debated internally for centuries. The debate has not produced a conclusion.

The specific prayer does not seem to matter. The specific religion does not seem to matter. What seems to matter, according to field reports, is whether the person means it, which is the same sincerity problem as holy water and equally unmeasurable.

Gordon does not pray.

## Iron

Professional assessment: genuinely uncomfortable. Prolonged contact with iron produces a sensation that has been described in field reports as "a persistent wrongness that does not localize," which is, notably, exactly what we are trying to produce in subjects. The irony of this has been noted in the literature and has not led to further investigation.

Iron door handles, iron railings, iron cookware: all minor operational considerations. Most modern homes have very little exposed iron. Gordon's house has cast-iron radiators in two rooms, which I route around.

## Exorcism

Professional assessment: the occupational hazard. A formal exorcism conducted by a trained practitioner with institutional backing is the one countermeasure that reliably disrupts field operations. The mechanism is, once again, unknown. The effect is real: forced withdrawal from the site, temporary disorientation, and a mandatory incident report that takes longer to fill out than the exorcism took to perform.

Irrelevant to Gordon's case. Gordon is secular. Nobody in his orbit is going to call a priest. If they did, the priest would find a quiet man in a house with good acoustics who is having trouble sleeping, and the priest would recommend rest and possibly a counselor, and the priest would leave, and I would go back to work.

## Dogs

Professional assessment: not a countermeasure, but frequently mistaken for one. Dogs notice operatives. They do not react to operatives. A dog will track an operative's position in the room with its eyes and then go back to sleep. This is occasionally unnerving for junior operatives who have not encountered it before. The dog is not a threat. The dog is a mild professional inconvenience, in that you have to work around its awareness without triggering a behavioral response (barking, alerting the subject) that would compromise the atmospheric build.

Gordon does not have a dog. He has considered getting one. I have seen the browser history.
