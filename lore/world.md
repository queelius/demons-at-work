## Canonical Decisions (2026-04-09)

### Hell's Physical Environment: Corporate Uncanny

Hell's working environment is a real-looking corporate office with details that are subtly, persistently, unnervingly wrong. Not fire and brimstone. Not overtly surreal. Just:

- Ceilings a few inches too low to be comfortable but not low enough to remark on
- Fluorescent lights that hum at a frequency no Earth fluorescent lights hum at
- Hallways that occasionally seem longer than they were yesterday
- A break room whose coffee is always exactly the wrong temperature
- Windows that show a view nobody has ever successfully described twice the same way
- Cubicles, standard office furniture, ergonomic chairs, interdepartmental memos, laminated emergency procedures

The horror of hell is that it looks like where you already work. Hell haunts its own employees with exactly the kind of wrongness it trains them to inflict on humans. No one in the office acknowledges this. Acknowledging it would not be career-advancing.

### The Commuter Model

The demon does not live on site at the haunting. The demon commutes.

Standard workday structure:
1. Arrive at hell's office. Check assignments. Attend mandatory briefings.
2. Transit to Earth (method TBD, probably something bureaucratic involving paperwork).
3. Work a shift at the target's house. Execute the scheduled atmospheric beats.
4. Transit back. File the shift report. Clock out.

The commute itself is narratively productive. It gives us two settings (the office, the house), two social environments (colleagues, solitude), and the transition between them. The comedy of punching out of cosmic evil at the end of a shift is a core tonal beat.

A single haunting may overlap with other logistical demands (meetings, performance reviews, training the new hire), because demons are not given one case at a time; they are expected to manage scheduling themselves within the assignment's constraints. This is where the "tedious professional job" register lives.

### The House (This Assignment's Setting)

The target's house is a 1920s bungalow or Craftsman on a quiet older residential street. Lived in for decades. Owned by the target and the spouse for most of their marriage. Not rural, not urban, not suburban in the new-build sense. The kind of house real estate listings call "full of character."

Physical qualities the demon's professional eye responds to:

- Hardwood floors that settle audibly, with specific creaks the demon can map and exploit.
- Plaster walls, not drywall. The acoustics are old-house acoustics.
- High ceilings and original woodwork. Built-in cabinetry and corners positioned for a demon to stand in without being seen.
- An attic with a pull-down ladder. A basement with an exterior entrance.
- Single-story or story-and-a-half, which gives the demon sight lines through the house from multiple vantage points.
- A back yard with a garden the spouse used to tend.

Physical details that carry the history of care (see characters.md for the target's backstory):

- A grab bar still installed in the hallway near the bedroom.
- A raised toilet seat in the main bathroom.
- An imprint in the bedroom floor where the hospital bed stood.
- A medicine cabinet still full of labeled bottles, mostly expired.
- A pair of armchairs by the fireplace, one moved slightly away from the other. One has a deep imprint. The other does not.
- Furniture slightly rearranged around absences.

The demon catalogs these details professionally in the early chapters as environmental features. The reader reads them as grief. This gap is the book's first engine of dramatic irony.

### The Theological Ambiguity Is the Point

Elaborated below in the original theological section, but lifted here because it is architecturally canonical: **nobody knows if God is real.** Nobody knows if the fear-to-damnation pipeline works. The bureaucracy runs on institutional faith, and questioning the premise is not career-advancing. The demon knows this, has known it for years, and finds it neither liberating nor devastating. It is the climate they work in.

This ambiguity is not a mystery the book resolves. It is the air the book breathes.

## Hell as Organization

Hell is a bureaucracy. Not metaphorically. It has departments, reporting structures, performance review cycles, and interdepartmental memos. The cosmological war between good and evil has, over the millennia, calcified into an organization that exists primarily to perpetuate itself.

### The Hierarchy (Rough Sketch)

Top-down, the structure might look like:

- **Upper Management:** Archdemons, princes, the old guard. Rarely seen. Issue directives that filter down as increasingly garbled policy. They may or may not have a strategic vision. Nobody at the working level has ever confirmed this.
- **Middle Management:** Department heads, regional coordinators. They translate upper management's vague mandates into specific assignments. They write the reports. They run the review cycles. They are the layer where "terrify the human into rejecting God" becomes "three weeks atmospheric dread, minimum two manifestations per week, escalating to physical interaction by week four."
- **Field Operatives:** The demons who do the actual haunting. Our protagonist lives here. Skilled, underappreciated, caught between impossible quotas and uncooperative humans.
- **Support Staff:** Whoever maintains the infrastructure. The bureaucratic machinery of assignment allocation, territory management, interdepartmental coordination. Possibly also literal maintenance (hell has facilities?).

### Departments (Possible)

If the book uses a Dante-inspired structure with different horror genres as departments:

| Department | Horror Genre | Specialty | Organizational Culture |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hauntings | Atmospheric/ghost | Long-game dread, environmental manipulation, the slow build | Artisanal. Craftspeople. Contemptuous of jump-scare departments. |
| Possessions | Exorcism/body horror | Direct neural takeover, voice modulation, contortion | Medical precision. They see themselves as surgeons. |
| Apparitions | Jump scare / visual | Brief, high-impact manifestations. The face in the window. | Flashy. High turnover. The marketing department of hell. |
| Whispers | Psychological horror | Intrusive thoughts, paranoia induction, trust erosion | Quiet, methodical. Long timelines. Hate being grouped with Hauntings. |
| Deals | Faustian / folk | Contract negotiation, wish corruption, fine print | Lawyerly. Consider themselves the intellectuals. |
| Slaughter | Slasher / physical | Direct violence, pursuit, the unstoppable figure | Blue collar. Other departments look down on them. "No finesse." |
| Cosmic | Lovecraftian / existential | Revelation of meaninglessness, geometry that shouldn't exist | PhD types. Obscure. Nobody else understands what they do or why. |

### The Assignment System

Hauntings are assigned. A field operative receives a case file: the target, the objective, the timeline, the approved escalation schedule. They don't get to choose their targets. They don't get to improvise freely. There are protocols.

But protocols are guidelines, and every field operative develops their own style within the constraints. The tension between protocol and personal craft is a source of both pride and friction.

### Performance Metrics

Demons are evaluated. The metrics are:

- **Fear Index:** Sustained terror level in the target, measured... somehow. Nobody agrees on the methodology.
- **Timeline Adherence:** Did the haunting progress through the approved phases on schedule?
- **Collateral Awareness:** Did neighbors, friends, or authorities become aware? (Bad: increases priest/intervention risk. Good: amplifies isolation.)
- **Soul Outcome:** The final metric. Did the target reject divine protection? Did the soul darken? This is the one that matters and the one nobody can reliably influence, which is the central absurdity of the entire enterprise.

### The Theological Problem Nobody Talks About

The official doctrine: haunting weakens the human's spiritual defenses, creating an opening for damnation. Fear leads to despair, despair leads to rejection of God, rejection of God leads to a harvestable soul.

The unofficial reality: there is no evidence this works. Most haunted humans call priests. Most possessions get exorcised. The ones who do "reject God" were probably going to anyway. The entire fear-to-damnation pipeline has never been validated, and questioning it is not career-advancing.

## The Human Side

The humans are not characters in the traditional sense. They're the *audience* for the demon's performance. They exist as seen through the demon's professional eye: obstacles, opportunities, uncooperative subjects.

But the comedy also comes from the gap between how the demon sees the human (a target, a canvas, a professional challenge) and what the human actually is (a person trying to live their life who has an unwanted demon in their house).

### Common Human Responses That Ruin Everything

- Calling a plumber instead of a priest
- Assuming the house has mice
- Getting noise-canceling headphones
- Moving
- Being too tired from work to notice the carefully orchestrated dread
- Having a dog that treats the demon as a mild curiosity
- Being on their phone during the manifestation

## The Earthside Operating Environment

The demon operates on Earth but is not of Earth. Practical questions:

- **Physicality:** Can the demon interact with objects? Presumably yes (moving things, writing messages, etc.) but within limits. The limits are part of the frustration.
- **Visibility:** The demon can choose when and how to be seen. The craft is in the calibration: barely visible, peripheral vision, just enough to register.
- **Time on site:** CANONICAL. The demon commutes from hell's office. See "The Commuter Model" above.

### The Caseload (Canonical 2026-04-11)

A field operative carries multiple cases simultaneously. Typically two to four, in various phases. One is usually the primary (most complex, most attention-demanding). The others are in earlier or later phases and require less intensive daily work. Phase 1 cases need a few hours of ambient setup per shift. Phase 4 cases need precision timing and extended on-site presence. The operative manages their own scheduling across cases.

There is no off-duty. The caseload fills the hours. Between house shifts, there is the office: reports, meetings, training, the break room coffee that is always the wrong temperature. Between office tasks, there is another house. The transit form always has a destination. The schedule is always full.

This is not coercion. Nobody chains an operative to the desk. It is structure. The work fills the available time because there is always more work, and the professional identity fills the self because there is nothing else competing for the space. The concept of "time off" may exist somewhere in the Operations Manual. Nobody has found the page.

**What this means for the book:**

Tert's life is: the office, the houses, the transit between them. There is no fourth place. The system did not take his personal life. It occupied the space where one would go, the way water fills a container, without force and without remainder.

Gordon's case (47-C) is Tert's primary assignment, the canvas, the one that matters. He has other cases. They are routine, unremarkable, professionally adequate. The book does not follow them because they are not the story. But they exist, and they are where Tert goes when he is not at Gordon's house, and the contrast between how Tert approaches the routine cases and how he approaches Gordon's becomes visible post-turn, not dramatically, just in the quality of attention. The routine cases get the standard work. Gordon's case gets whatever Gordon's case has become.

**The thematic parallel:** Gordon's life was consumed by caregiving and then by grief. His identity was "husband" and "caregiver" and both are gone. Tert's life is consumed by the work. His identity is "operative." The question "who are you when the defining structure of your life is removed?" applies to both of them. The book removes Gordon's structure (his wife died). It does not remove Tert's. Tert ends the book still inside the system. The reader is left with the question the book does not answer: if the work stopped, who would Tert be?

## Open Questions

Remaining after the 2026-04-09 architecture pass.

1. **Can demons die or be hurt?** Are exorcisms a genuine occupational hazard? Our target is secular, so a formal exorcism is unlikely in this book, but the background threat matters for worldbuilding. Tentative lean: injury is possible, death is not (or is rare enough to be a career-ending horror story colleagues tell).
2. **What's the economy?** Do demons get paid? In what? What do they spend it on? Low priority, but small specific details in this space are comedy gold (the break room vending machine, a retirement plan).
3. **The departments list.** The seven-department table is still speculative. For the novella we mostly need Hauntings (where Tert works) and Apparitions (for contrast). Others are texture.
