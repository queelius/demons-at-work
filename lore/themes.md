## Canonical Decisions (2026-04-09)

The novella has a shape. These decisions are locked in.

### Scope

Novelette. One haunting. Briefing to case closure. ~13,600 words. Not a novel, not a collection, not a tour of departments. Other departments and the wider bureaucracy enrich the world *around* this single assignment; they are not the structure.

### Arc: Craft Over Outcome

The demon executes a masterful haunting of a target they cannot break, realizes this around the midpoint, and finishes the work anyway because the craft is the point. Not triumphant, not tragic. The quiet dignity of doing meaningless work well.

### The Kernel

**The demon is haunting someone who's already haunted.**

The target is a widow or widower, eight to twelve months into grief for their spouse. The house already feels wrong to them because of real loss. The demon's manufactured dread layers onto real sadness, and the target attributes all of it to grief. The demon's best work is invisible, misattributed, folded into a narrative the demon cannot touch. The absent spouse creates a presence in the house the demon isn't, competing for the wrongness the demon is trying to produce, and winning.

### Theological Stance

Genuinely ambiguous. Nobody in hell's hierarchy knows if God is real, if souls are harvestable, or if the fear-to-damnation pipeline actually works. The bureaucracy operates on institutional faith, not evidence. The demon knows the mechanism is unvalidated and does the work anyway. This ambiguity is not a mystery to resolve over the course of the book; it is the satirical architecture the book sits on.

### The Sisyphus Principle

One must imagine Sisyphus happy. This is the book's philosophical spine.

Tert knows the boulder goes back down. He has always known. He does the work anyway, and he does it well, and he has made that into something livable through irony and craft. The absurd hero, clocking in.

Gordon also knows the boulder goes back down. His wife is not coming back. The book will end. The empty room will stay empty. He reads to it anyway.

The turn is Tert watching Gordon and seeing what his own answer to Camus's question looks like without the irony. Just the devotion. Just the boulder. And it does not look absurd.

### The Real Subject (Canonical 2026-04-11)

The premise is horror-comedy seen from inside. The book is about something else.

It is about what it means to do harmful work well. To be a skilled professional inside a system whose purpose you cannot justify, whose metrics measure nothing real, and whose output causes harm to a person you can see if you look closely enough. To cope through irony and craft. To take pride in your piece while knowing the whole is indefensible. To go back to work on Monday.

This is not a niche experience. Defense engineers, surveillance developers, pharma marketers, fossil fuel geologists, and everyone who has ever done skilled work inside a system whose net effect they would not choose if they could see it whole. The book is about what that looks like from inside, not from outside where you can judge.

The demon frame strips away the self-justifications. In the real world, people tell themselves stories: "weapons deter war," "the drug helps some patients," "if not me, someone else." These stories insulate. Tert has none of them. The pipeline is unvalidated. The Fear Index is nonsense. There is no story Tert can tell himself about the work being justified. The book removes the insulation layer and shows what is underneath: craft, irony, proximity, continuation.

What the book argues:

1. **The system recruits talent by offering interesting problems.** The bookshelves, the acoustics, the room that was accidentally tuned: this is the hook. The better Tert is at the work, the more effective the system is at causing harm. His craft devotion IS his complicity. Sophisticated harmful institutions give talented people meaningful work at the individual level while serving a harmful purpose at the systemic level.

2. **The system absorbs resistance.** Eleven minutes becomes "thermal conditions." Meri's questions get deflected with Management language. Prim's careful reading surfaces a pattern and converts it into a supplemental thermal survey. Every act of seeing, questioning, or deviating gets translated back into the system's categories. The forms always come out looking like the system is working.

3. **Awareness changes the person without changing the system.** The turn does not redeem Tert. It does not lead to rebellion or refusal. It makes the work heavier. The irony is thinner. The forms no longer contain the reality. Whether awareness without action is meaningful or merely comfortable is the reader's question. The book holds it open.

4. **Choice is deliberately ambiguous.** Can Tert quit? Transfer? Refuse? The book does not say, because in the real world, choice within a system is complicated. Whether Tert could leave matters less than whether leaving would change anything. If he quits, someone else haunts Gordon. The harm is structural.

### The Inner Life the Genre Denies (Canonical 2026-04-11)

Horror movies do not give demons an inner life. The demon exists in service to the scare. No backstage, no off-screen, no interiority. Its life begins when the lights flicker and ends when the credits roll.

This is exactly what the system does to the worker. Your existence is in service to the output. The system does not care what you think or feel. It cares what you produce.

The book's project is to give the demon the inner life that both the genre and the system deny. Not as a sentimental correction ("actually, demons have feelings") but as an investigation: what IS the inner life of a being whose entire existence is structured by work? The answer: the inner life develops inside the work, because there is nowhere else for it to go. Tert's interiority is craft, irony, observation, and eventually the awareness of another person that neither the genre nor the system has any use for.

Gordon is the mirror. Gordon is also consumed by a structure (grief). His routines orbit a person who is not there. But Gordon creates moments of interiority inside the structure: the garden, the reading, the laugh at 4 AM. Gordon pushes back against the thing that consumes him, not by escaping it but by making something inside it.

Tert has never had to do this. His structure has never been challenged. The work fills the space and the space has always been enough. The Middlemarch scene is the moment Tert sees someone else's interiority and realizes his own has been the shape of the container it grew in.

The horror-genre meta-commentary (the original premise) and the complicity commentary (the evolved theme) are the same argument at different levels. The genre denies the demon a self. The system denies the worker a self. The book asks what happens when the self shows up anyway.

### Tonal Commitment

Comedy AND genuine pathos. The grief is real. The target is real. Tert's irony and Gordon's sincerity are two responses to the same condition, and the book holds them side by side without choosing between them. If the pathos isn't there, the craft-over-outcome arc doesn't land. If the comedy isn't there, the book collapses into a different, heavier novella we're not writing.

The dual register is the hardest thing to execute and the most important.

## The Core Joke

Horror movies make no sense from the monster's perspective. The demon has the power to drag you to hell, but instead spends six weeks flickering lights, whispering your name from empty rooms, and crouching in dark corners for hours waiting for you to glance over. Why?

The book takes this question seriously. Not to answer it (there is no good answer, that's the joke) but to inhabit the demon's POV and let the absurdity unfold from the inside.

## Primary Themes

### 1. The Absurdity of Evil as a Profession

Scaring humans is a job. It has procedures, expectations, performance metrics, and a bureaucracy. The demon doesn't get to just *be* evil. They have to execute evil according to protocols that nobody can justify but everyone follows. The horror genre's conventions become workplace policy.

This is the satirical core: evil, when professionalized, becomes indistinguishable from any other dysfunctional organization. The same middle-management mediocrity, the same cargo-cult adherence to processes nobody understands the purpose of, the same gap between what the job is supposed to achieve and what anyone actually does day-to-day.

### 2. The Craft of Terror (and Its Pointlessness)

The demon is genuinely skilled. They have aesthetic opinions. They know the difference between a cheap jump scare and a properly built atmospheric dread sequence. They take pride in the work.

The comedy is that the craft is exquisite and the strategic value is zero. It's like being the world's best calligrapher in the age of email. The demon has perfected an art form that serves no discernible purpose, and they know it, and they do it anyway, because what else are they going to do?

### 3. The Motivation Gap

The stated purpose of haunting (reject God, damn the soul, weaken the victim's will, etc.) has no logical connection to the actual activities (making floorboards creak, appearing briefly in mirrors, leaving cryptic messages in condensation). The demon knows this. The hierarchy insists the connection exists. Nobody can explain the mechanism.

This mirrors every corporate strategy deck that claims Activity X leads to Outcome Y through a mechanism nobody has ever validated.

### 4. The Internal Monologue

What does a demon think about while crouched motionless in a dark corner for four hours? While holding a fixed expression of menace in case someone opens a closet door? While carefully arranging a child's toys in a circle at 3 AM?

The answer is: the same things anyone thinks about during tedious skilled labor. Their back hurts. They're bored. They're rehearsing. They're second-guessing creative choices. They're thinking about what they're going to do after this assignment. They're having small existential crises about whether any of this matters.

### 5. The Theatre of Evil

Horror is *performance*. The demon is an actor, a director, a set designer, and a lighting technician, all performing for an audience of one who didn't buy a ticket and would very much like to leave. The demon's "scary sounds" are rehearsed. The "menacing eyes barely visible in the dark corner" is a *choice* -- they positioned themselves there, they calibrated the eye-glow, they waited.

The horror genre, seen from the performer's side, is community theatre with existential stakes.

## Secondary Themes

### Hierarchy and Bureaucracy

Hell has a chain of command. Someone assigns hauntings. Someone reviews performance. There are quotas, reports, interdepartmental politics. The demon is not a free agent of chaos. They are an employee.

### Pride in Pointless Work

The demon cares about quality even though the work doesn't achieve anything. This is weirdly sympathetic. They're not evil in any grand cosmic sense. They're a craftsperson in a broken system.

### The Human as Uncooperative Audience

The victim doesn't follow the script. They call plumbers. They rationalize. They put in headphones. They get a dog. The demon's carefully orchestrated campaign keeps getting derailed by mundane human practicality.

### Dante as Precursor

The *Inferno* established hell as a bureaucratic system with organizational structure, jurisdictional boundaries, and assigned roles. This book updates that tradition: Dante's precisely ordered circles of hell become a modern dysfunctional workplace. The medieval cosmological precision becomes corporate process documentation. The contrapasso (punishment fitting the crime) becomes KPIs that don't measure anything real.

## Tone

**Dry humor.** Not wacky, not zany, not parodic. Tert is a fully self-aware ironist who sees the absurdity of the entire enterprise, including his own place in it. The reader is with Tert, not ahead of him. The comedy comes from sharing Tert's deadpan observations about a system that is absurd and that he participates in anyway.

Think: the deadpan of *The Office* applied to demonic haunting, crossed with the moral weight of a worker who knows exactly what they are doing and does it well. Or *What We Do in the Shadows* if it took the "why are you doing this?" question seriously enough to discover it has no comfortable answer.

The horror elements should be *real*, genuinely well-executed set pieces, but seen from backstage. The reader gets the scare AND the behind-the-scenes, simultaneously. Post-turn, the reader also gets the cost.

---

## What This Book Is NOT

- Not a parody that makes fun of horror. It loves horror. It just notices that the genre makes no sense from the other side.
- Not whimsical or cartoonish. The demon is not bumbling. He is competent and thoughtful and the work is still absurd.
- Not a strict allegory for any single institution. The parallels to corporate work, military service, and institutional complicity are real and intentional, but the book maps onto the *experience* of being inside a harmful system, not onto one particular system.
- Not a redemption arc. Tert does not become good. He does not quit. He does not rebel. He goes back to work with the awareness, and the awareness is all the growth there is.
- Not grimdark. The humor is the point. If a chapter is not funny, it is failing. But if the only thing a chapter does is be funny, it is also failing. The comedy and the complicity commentary operate simultaneously.

## Open Questions

Remaining tactical questions as of 2026-04-11.

1. **Gordon's last name and his wife's name.** Tactical. No load-bearing choice here beyond "ordinary, not symbolic."
2. **Gordon's social world.** Does anyone visit the house during the five weeks? If so, how does Tert observe/handle the visit? Low priority for the remaining chapters.
3. **The specific content of Meri's final statement.** Meri makes a statement, not a question, in the last office scene. What do they say? It should show Meri has been watching Tert the way Tert watches Gordon.
4. **The new case file detail.** One human detail in the next assignment that Tert pauses on. Specific enough to be concrete, ordinary enough to be universal. The pause is the ending.
