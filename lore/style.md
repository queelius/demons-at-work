# Style Guide

**Status:** Largely CANONICAL (2026-04-09); minor details still open

---

## The Dual Register

**This is the hardest thing the book has to do and the most important.**

The novella operates in two registers simultaneously: dry workplace comedy and genuine pathos about a grieving person. Neither register can swamp the other.

- When the demon is at the office, being briefed, filing reports, dealing with the Manager or the New Hire, the book is workplace comedy. Dry. Observational. The satirical architecture does the work.
- When the demon is at the house watching the target, the book is literary fiction with a horror framework. The grief is real. The noticing is real. The demon's professional eye picks up things the target does not even know they are doing, and the reader picks them up through the demon.
- The horror set pieces must still be genuine horror. Effective, well-crafted, atmospheric. The demon is good at their job.
- The comedy must stay dry. Sincere. Never mugging. The demon does not know they are funny and does not know they are seeing something tender. Both of these are true and both are invisible to the narrator.

The reader is in a position the demon is not: seeing the comedy and the pathos at the same time. The voice is the demon's; the register shifts happen in what the voice notices, not in how the voice speaks.

### What this means in practice

- No sentimentality. The grief is never written for tears. It is written for recognition.
- No jokes at the target's expense. The demon has professional frustrations with the target's "uncooperativeness," but the target is never the butt of the humor. The bureaucracy is the butt.
- The comic beats and the tender beats can share scenes. A scene in which the demon is annoyed that the target fell asleep during a carefully calibrated atmospheric sequence should also be, quietly, a scene about how exhausted the target is.

## Voice

**First person, present tense.** The demon narrates. Dry, observational, professional. They describe their work the way a skilled tradesperson describes their trade: with specificity, opinion, and quiet pride.

### Irony, Craft, and the Blind Spot (Canonical 2026-04-10)

Tert is a jaded office worker who copes with the absurdity of his occupation by embracing it. He weaponizes irony. He circles "hat" on the transit form. He knows the Fear Index is nonsense, that the escalation templates are written by people who have never done field work, that the fear-to-damnation pipeline has never been validated, that "above my operational scope" is a dodge. He sees the whole enterprise clearly and has made his peace with it the way intelligent people in dysfunctional organizations do: by being very good at his specific piece of the work and letting the rest be someone else's problem.

The irony and the craft coexist. Tert mocks the system and takes the work seriously, simultaneously, without experiencing this as a contradiction. This is the key to the voice. He is not an earnest craftsman who happens to work in an absurd system (that character would be oblivious). He is a jaded ironist who also happens to care deeply about atmospheric horror as a discipline (that character is more interesting and more human).

**Tert is fully self-aware.** He knows the enterprise is absurd. He knows his craft devotion within it is absurd. He knows he is a demon who spends four hours crouched behind a dresser and that this is ridiculous. He has made his peace with all of it. Irony lets him see clearly and keep going. This is not a character who needs the reader to notice something on his behalf. The reader and Tert are on the same page about the comedy.

**What changes at the turn is not awareness. It is resonance.** Tert watches Gordon read Middlemarch aloud to an empty room, one evening a week, continuing a book his dead wife will never finish, and recognizes something he was not expecting to recognize. Not a new fact. A new feeling. Someone else devoting themselves to something in the face of futility, and it does not look absurd from the outside. It looks like love.

Tert has always known what his own situation looks like. He has never seen it on someone else's face. The turn is the joke landing somewhere other than his sense of humor.

The comedy operates on one level with the reader, not two against the reader:
- **Tert's irony about everything, including himself** (which he shares with the reader through deadpan observation, small acts of subversion, and professional self-awareness). The reader is not ahead of Tert. The reader is with Tert.
- **The pathos arrives when irony stops being enough.** Gordon's ritual is the thing Tert's irony cannot metabolize. Not because Tert suddenly learns something, but because knowing you are part of the joke and feeling the weight of it are different experiences, and the second one is new.

### Tone Calibration

- **Not wacky.** The demon is not a comedian. They are a professional with a dry sense of irony describing their profession. The comedy is situational and observational, not performative.
- **Not bitter.** The demon is not ranting about hell. They have complaints (who doesn't) but they show up and do the work.
- **Fully self-aware jaded ironist who still cares.** Tert sees the absurdity of the entire enterprise, including his own place in it, and copes by weaponizing irony. He also takes the craft seriously. He knows this combination is itself absurd. He has made his peace with it. The reader is not ahead of Tert. The reader is with him.
- **Genuinely knowledgeable.** When the demon talks about the craft of scaring, it should read like a master discussing their art. The reader should learn something about why horror works, even as they laugh at the context.

### Example Register (Rough)

"You don't start with the apparition. That's amateur hour. You start with the sounds. Settling-house sounds, but wrong. The creak that happens at 3 AM when nobody's walking. The tap that runs for two seconds when the handle hasn't moved. You build a foundation of wrongness. The human doesn't know what's wrong yet. They just know something is. That's your opening. You're not scaring them. You're making them available to be scared. There's a difference, and if you don't understand the difference, you're in the wrong department."

### What the Voice Is NOT

- Not Pratchett (too knowing, too meta)
- Not Adams (too whimsical, too digressive)
- Not Palahniuk (too aggressive, too confrontational)
- Closer to: a very dry Christopher Guest mockumentary, but in prose. Or the narrator of a Coen Brothers film who happens to be the demon.

## The Horror Scenes

The horror set pieces should be **genuinely well-crafted horror** when described from the human's perspective. The demon is good at this. When they describe what they did, the reader should feel the chill.

Then the demon explains why they made the creative choices they made, and the chill becomes comedy.

The dual effect (actually scary, also funny) is the book's signature. If the horror isn't good, the comedy doesn't land. The demon's expertise must be real for their situation to be absurd.

### Technique: The Setup/Craft Reveal

1. Describe the scare as the human experiences it (atmospheric, effective, genuinely creepy)
2. Cut to the demon's internal monologue during the scare (bored, in pain from holding still, debating whether the eye-glow is too much)
3. The demon's post-scare analysis (what worked, what didn't, what they'd do differently)

The reader oscillates between "that's terrifying" and "this poor demon has been crouching behind a dresser for four hours."

---

## Pacing

Short chapters. The demon's narration is clipped, professional. Long atmospheric passages only during the actual scare descriptions, where the demon's craft gets room to breathe. Then back to the dry commentary.

The rhythm: setup (brief, dry) -> scare (atmospheric, extended) -> aftermath (dry, analytical, funny).

---

## Dialogue

Dialogue between demons should read like coworkers. Shop talk. Complaining about management. Comparing techniques. Professional jealousy.

The demon does not speak to humans during the haunting (that would break the mystique). Any "demonic" communication (whispers, written messages, etc.) is described from the demon's side as a deliberate creative act with specific intent behind every word choice.

---

## Anti-Cliche Rules

- The demon never describes themselves as "evil." They describe themselves as doing their job.
- No "mwahaha" energy. No villain monologues. No gleeful sadism.
- Hell is not "edgy." It's bureaucratic. The horror of hell is meetings, not fire.
- Humans are not stupid. They're rational actors dealing with irrational circumstances. The comedy is that rationality defeats the demon's best work.
- No breaking the fourth wall. The demon narrates sincerely. They don't wink at the reader.
