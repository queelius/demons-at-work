# Outline

**Status:** High-level arc CANONICAL (2026-04-09); scene-level detail still EXPLORATORY

The novella's arc is locked in at the structural level. Specific scenes, chapter breaks, and the escalation schedule of the haunting itself are still being designed.

---

## Shape

A novella of roughly 25 to 30 thousand words. First person, present tense. One haunting, briefing to case closure.

Three movements:

1. **Setup and execution** (0 to roughly 60 percent). The assignment briefing. The demon reads the case file and sees the house as the canvas they have been waiting for. The haunting begins. Early atmospheric beats. The office scenes in parallel: the Manager, the New Hire, the bureaucracy. The target is rendered through the demon's professional eye; the reader slowly accumulates the sense that this person is grieving something real.
2. **The turn** (roughly 60 percent). The demon witnesses the target's weekly ritual of continuity. Something tips over. The demon realizes the target will not break, and has been watching the target for long enough that the recognition of this is no longer purely professional.
3. **Completion** (roughly 60 to 100 percent). The demon finishes the haunting. Not half-heartedly. Better than before. The craft becomes its own justification. The escalation schedule continues on protocol. The reader watches a masterwork being executed in full knowledge of its futility. The tonal register quietly deepens.

## Endpoints

### Opening

Page one, the demon receives the case file. Reads it. Has opinions. The bureaucratic context and the demon's voice establish simultaneously. The voice lands in the first paragraph because the demon is doing something utterly mundane (paperwork) about something cosmic (destroying a soul).

The case file is the book's first framing device: it tells the reader what the haunting is supposed to accomplish, on paper. The rest of the book is what actually happens.

### Ending

Back at the office. The demon closes the file. Transit back from the target's house for the last time. Files the final report in standard bureaucratic language that contains none of what the book has just shown the reader. Receives the next assignment. Sits down with it.

Visibly unchanged. But there is one small moment: a pause, a lingering look at something, a beat the demon did not take in the opening. The reader notices it. The demon does not name it.

The final image is the demon reading a new case file with the same professional attention they brought to the first one, before the book started. The ambiguity of whether anything has changed is the final note.

## The Turn in Detail

The 60 percent pivot is the book's structural and emotional center. It must be earned by the first 60 percent, not announced.

Prerequisites the first movement must establish:

- The demon's pride in the craft, demonstrated through specific technical choices and the demon's internal commentary.
- The target's situation, shown through the demon's observation rather than stated. The reader should understand that the target is grieving before the word "grief" is used.
- The absence of the spouse in the house, rendered through objects, routines, and spaces the target has not been able to bring themselves to rearrange.
- The demon's awareness that the haunting is going exactly to plan and that the plan is not translating into results.

The turn itself: Tert witnesses Gordon reading Middlemarch aloud. He watches from the hallway. He does not articulate what he is feeling because his irony, which has always been sufficient, is not sufficient for this. He is watching someone else devote themselves to something futile with complete sincerity, and it does not look absurd from the outside.

After the turn, Tert keeps working at full capacity. He has not lost his irony. He has not had a conversion. But the distance between himself and the work has quietly closed, and he does not know how to reopen it.

## What the Middle Does

The third quarter of the book, between the turn and the ending, is where the novella is most at risk. Tert's irony is still intact but no longer doing its job as insulation. The comedy risks becoming depressive; the pathos risks becoming monotonous.

What gets us through this stretch:

- The New Hire becomes more important here. Their questions escalate in specificity. Tert's answers have shifted slightly, not in content but in weight. The deflections come a beat later. The irony is still there but it is working harder.
- The Manager delivers a performance review or a mid-cycle check-in. The gap between the official metrics and what the demon is actually experiencing becomes unignorable in the bureaucratic register. This is where the satire sharpens.
- The haunting itself enters its most technically demanding phase. The craft is at its peak. The reader gets the best atmospheric set pieces of the book.
- Small observations of the target accumulate. Not scenes. Moments. The texture of a life.

## Five-Week Structure (Canonical 2026-04-09)

The haunting runs five weeks, matching hell's standard atmospheric haunting cycle. Each week is a phase. The in-world protocols assume this cadence; the novella inherits it.

### Week 1: Ambient Setup

Environmental wrongness at the floor level. Settling-house sounds that are not settling. Small temperature shifts. A sense that something is off without anything nameable. Gordon notices nothing specific; the demon catalogs the house in detail and begins positioning.

Office parallel: the demon receives the assignment (chapter 1), attends the initial briefing, interacts with the Manager, starts filing shift reports. The New Hire is introduced.

### Week 2: Specific Local Manifestations

Individual beats. A tap that runs for two seconds in an empty bathroom. A book on the floor that was on a shelf. A shadow briefly wrong at the edge of a doorway. Gordon starts to notice. He attributes everything to grief, to being tired, to the house settling, to stress. The demon is satisfied with the work.

Office parallel: week-two review cadence. The Manager delivers bureaucratic encouragement. The New Hire asks a question the demon cannot answer cleanly.

### Week 3: The Turn

The haunting continues to schedule. The demon's work is at technical peak. Mid-week (likely a Sunday evening, the standing time Gordon reads), the demon witnesses the weekly ritual: Gordon picking up Middlemarch, settling into his chair, starting where the bookmark was, reading aloud. The demon watches from the hallway and understands what they are seeing.

The moment is not a realization. Tert already knows everything there is to know about the absurdity of his work. What happens is not cognitive but emotional: he watches someone else devote themselves to something futile with complete sincerity, and his irony cannot metabolize it. It does not look absurd from the outside. It looks like love. This is the thing that is new.

The rest of week three continues on schedule. Tert keeps working. The shift is not in his attention or his craft. It is in the distance between him and the work, which has quietly closed.

Office parallel: a mid-cycle check-in. The gap between the official metrics and what the demon is actually seeing becomes sharp.

### Week 4: Full Escalation

The most technically demanding phase. Direct manifestations. Sound design at peak. Positioning at peak. The demon's best set pieces of the book land here. The craft is being performed in full knowledge that it will not work, and the craft is better for it. This is where the book earns the craft-over-outcome arc.

Office parallel: the New Hire's questions escalate. The protagonist's answers have changed slightly in ways the New Hire cannot quite identify.

### Week 5: Resolution and Case Closure

The final sequence, per hell's protocol for bringing an atmospheric haunting to its "completion state." This is the moment the protocol calls for the soul outcome to be determined. The demon files the final shift reports. Closes the case. Transits back to the office one last time.

Office: the file is closed. The next assignment arrives. The final image (see Endpoints section above).

## Chapter Structure (Canonical 2026-04-09)

Short, variable-length. 15 to 20 chapters. Most in the 1,000 to 3,000 word range. Some shorter when a moment calls for it (a single observation, a single beat). Chapters alternate between office and house but not mechanically; the pattern should feel organic to the demon's actual workday. Cluster office scenes when bureaucratic pressure is building. Cluster house scenes during the haunting's most technical phases.

Speculative chapter distribution (subject to drafting):

1. The briefing. Office. Demon reads the case file. First voice beat.
2. First visit to the house. Commute. Initial environmental cataloging.
3. The house itself, examined. The demon's professional awe at the canvas.
4. Week 1 begins. First atmospheric beats. Gordon's first evening post-setup.
5. Office interlude. First shift report. The Manager. Introduction of the New Hire.
6. Week 2 opens. Local manifestations. The tap.
7. Gordon's week two: noticing, attributing to grief.
8. Office: the Manager delivers a mid-cycle review. Quotas. Corporate-speak.
9. A short chapter: the demon watching Gordon do something small and tender.
10. Week 3 arrives. The demon at technical peak.
11. The turn. The Sunday evening. Middlemarch. The demon in the hallway.
12. The rest of week 3, rendered differently. Nothing the demon does has visibly changed. Everything has.
13. Week 4 opens. Office: the New Hire asks a question.
14. Full escalation. The book's best horror set pieces.
15. A quiet house chapter: the demon and the target in the same room, both alone.
16. Week 5: completion sequence. Final shifts.
17. Case closure. Office. The final report (bureaucratic, empty of everything we have seen).
18. The next assignment. The final image.

Subject to total and complete revision in drafting, but the shape is right.

## Open Structural Questions

1. **The New Hire's arc.** Do they have their own small arc within the book? Do they ever see the protagonist clearly? Do they get disillusioned too, faster or slower? Probably they stay earnest throughout, which makes the protagonist's lost earnestness more visible by contrast. But a case could be made for a late scene where the New Hire has a small doubt of their own.
2. **Gordon's social world inside the five weeks.** Who comes to the house? Anyone? A former colleague? A neighbor? A visit would be a scene the demon has to work around, and the demon's observation of Gordon with another person would add another register to our portrait of him. Probably one visit in the five weeks, probably late.
3. **The Manager's faith.** Is the Manager a true believer in the metrics, or a cynic performing belief? The novella can use either, but the reader should be able to tell which. Lean: true believer, because the sad comedy is sharper that way.
4. **The demon's transit method.** Bureaucratically mundane (a signed form, a specific door, a fluorescent-lit corridor nobody else uses). Should be gestured at early so we know the logistics.
5. **How much of the book is set in hell's office.** Rough estimate: 35 to 40 percent. The majority is at the house, but the office scenes are load-bearing for the satire.
