# Characters

**Status:** Protagonist and target CANONICAL (2026-04-09); specifics still EXPLORATORY

---

## The Protagonist

A mid-career field operative in the Hauntings department. Not a newbie, not a legend. Someone who has been doing this long enough to have opinions and short enough to still care about quality.

### Canonical (2026-04-09)

- **Commuter demon, multiple cases.** Based out of hell's office. Carries two to four cases simultaneously in various phases. Transits to whichever house needs attention that shift. Comes back. Files reports. Transits to the next house. Gordon's case (47-C) is the primary, the canvas, the one that matters. The others are routine, unremarkable, professionally adequate. The book follows Gordon's case; the others are referenced in passing.
- **Atmospheric horror specialist.** Considers themselves an artisan, or at least a competent craftsperson. Has strong aesthetic opinions. Contempt for cheap tactics (jump scares, excessive gore, "the Apparitions guys").
- **Aware of the motivation gap.** Knows the fear-to-damnation pipeline is unvalidated. Has made peace with this by not thinking about it at work. It is above their pay grade and there is no upside to dwelling on it.
- **Nickname: Tert.** The demon's formal designation is "Operator, Atmospheric Hauntings, Tertius Gradus" (or equivalent Latinate bureaucratese). Colleagues shortened the rank to "Tert." The demon never chose this. It stuck. They use it because not using it would be more conspicuous than using it. The Latinate roots of the designation nod to hell's ancient institutional bones retrofitted with modern office culture: the bureaucracy has old scaffolding and new cubicles.
- **Masterwork orientation in this assignment.** The house they have been assigned this time is a perfect atmospheric canvas. The demon sees it as a chance to do the kind of work they have been quietly preparing for their entire career. This is why they bring their A-game to an assignment the outcome of which turns out not to matter.
- **No self-pity.** They do not complain about being a demon. It is what they are. The grievances are professional (quotas, management, the new hire's questions), not existential.

### Still Open

- Gender (if demons have one at all). Low priority. The manuscript uses "he" in the lore but first-person narration avoids the question. Other characters have not used a pronoun for Tert yet.
- Backstory (ex-human? always a demon? does it matter?). The book gestures at this without resolving it. Unknown and not important. Tert is not the kind of person who asks.
- **There is no off-duty life.** The caseload fills the hours. Between house shifts, there is the office. Between office tasks, there is another house. The transit form always has a destination. Tert's life is: the office, the houses, the transit between them. There is no fourth place. This is not coercion; it is structure. The work fills the available space the way water fills a container, without force and without remainder. See world.md, "The Caseload."

### Voice Notes

The protagonist's narration should reveal:

- Deep knowledge of horror craft. They know why things scare people at a psychological level, and they can explain it without sounding like a textbook.
- Professional pride that borders on obsession.
- A dry fatalism about the organizational context. Not bitter. Not ranting. Just someone who has accepted how things are.
- Occasional flashes of something like empathy for the target. Not articulated as such. Quickly rationalized as a professional observation.
- No self-pity. No winking at the reader. No mugging. Tert is a fully self-aware jaded ironist who copes with the absurdity of the job by embracing it. He mocks the system, takes the craft seriously, and knows this combination is itself absurd. The reader is not ahead of Tert. They are with him. The turn is not a discovery. It is the moment his irony stops being enough to metabolize what he is seeing.

## The Target

**Gordon** (last name TBD). Male. 55 years old. Recently retired high school English teacher. Widower. His wife died roughly ten months ago after a long decline from ALS. He retired early to care for her.

Decent. Pragmatic. Secular. Not religious enough to find holy water meaningful, not afraid enough to reach for a priest. Treats the house's wrongness as an expression of his own grief, because that is the only frame available to him.

Seen entirely through the demon's eyes. We get the demon's professional assessment, not Gordon's interiority. But the demon is a careful observer, and what they notice accumulates into a portrait the demon cannot help building.

### Canonical (2026-04-09)

- **Gordon, 55, retired English teacher.** The name and profession matter because they give the reading-aloud ritual its weight. Gordon spent his career teaching books to children. He is now reading a book aloud to an empty room, for himself and for his wife and for the habit of reading to someone.
- **Recently widowed.** Ten months out from his wife's death. Sharp enough to still be raw, long enough that rebuilding has started.
- **His wife died of ALS.** A long decline. Gordon was the primary caregiver during the last years. The grief is settled and textured, not shocked. It includes exhaustion and a complicated relief Gordon does not allow himself to feel directly.
- **The house carries the history of care.** Years of quiet adjustments the demon notices and the target no longer sees: a grab bar still in the hallway, a raised toilet seat no one has removed, a stack of medication logs in a drawer, the imprint in the floor where the hospital bed used to be, a recliner positioned for a view the spouse liked. The demon's professional eye catalogs these. The reader gets the target's history through the demon's observation of the room.
- **Alone in the house they shared.** The house already has the emotional texture the demon is trying to manufacture. The absent spouse is a presence the demon cannot compete with.
- **Secular and pragmatic.** No religious framework. No belief in demons. When things in the house feel wrong, the explanation is always grief or coincidence or a bad night.
- **Kind.** Not dramatically so. Ordinarily decent. Does small things for other people. Maintains connections. Generates small daily meaning at a rate the demon cannot degrade fast enough to catch up with.
- **Weekly ritual: reads Middlemarch aloud.** Middlemarch was the book his wife was reading when she died. One evening a week, Gordon picks it up, starts where the bookmark was, and reads aloud. He moves through it slowly, the way she would have. He has been doing this for months; he is somewhere in the middle of the novel. The demon witnesses this from the hallway around the 60% mark of the book and understands only slowly what they are seeing: a weekly appointment with an unfinished conversation. The physical book (a thick paperback, old cover, dog-eared) is something the demon cataloged as environmental furniture in week one without recognizing what it was.
- **Attributes everything to grief.** The demon's best work does land, barely, late in the book, but Gordon folds it into his own narrative ("I've been hearing things lately," "the house feels different since...") and the demon's entire craft becomes invisible through misattribution.

### Still Open

- Last name. Something ordinary, not symbolic. Candidates: Ashworth, Caldwell, Hayes, Miller, Pemberton. Picking this is a minor decision that can happen in drafting.
- The wife's name. She will be named at least once, probably in the case file and possibly aloud when Gordon reads to her. Should be period-appropriate for a woman born ~1970.
- Social world. Friends, colleagues from the school, a neighbor, family nearby. Probably drawn sparingly. The most important questions: does Gordon still see anyone from work? Does anyone come to the house during the five weeks of the haunting?
- School. Which grade, which subject. Lean: high school English, probably 11th or 12th grade, teaching the kind of books a demon would recognize (the demon would note this with surprise; we and the demon share the recognition that Gordon has spent his life making young people love difficult books).

## Supporting Cast

CANONICAL: the novella supports two recurring supporting demons. The Manager (bureaucratic pressure) and the New Hire (unanswerable questions). Together they mirror the protagonist from above and below: the system that constrains them, and the self they used to be.

### The Manager: Prim

Designated Primus Ordinatus. Everyone shortens it to Prim. It suits them.

Tert's direct report. Issues assignments, reviews performance, delivers quotas. Speaks in corporate euphemism. "I wanted to touch base." "That is good field judgment." "Keep me posted." Carries a clipboard everywhere and a mug that says WORLD'S BEST and nothing else.

Prim believes in process the way a legal professional believes in process. Not because the outcomes are just. Not because the pipeline works. Because process is the thing that prevents the system from being arbitrary. Without the forms, the documentation, the flagged modifications, the review cadence, field operatives do whatever they want and evaluations become whims. The process is what makes the organization accountable to itself, even when "itself" is a bureaucracy whose stated purpose has never been validated.

Prim reads the forms because reading the forms matters. They fill in every field because the field exists for a reason, and even if nobody can remember the reason, skipping it is how standards erode. This is not naivete. It is discipline. A public defender who files every motion on time, in a system whose outcomes they may violently disagree with, because the process is what protects people when nothing else will.

**Prim's Sisyphean struggle is arguably more tragic than Tert's.** Tert at least has the craft. He chose his boulder and it gives him something back (the work itself, the aesthetics, the room with perfect acoustics). Prim's boulder is procedural integrity in an organization that does not deserve it. There is no beauty in a completed form. There is only correctness.

**Never write Prim as a punchline.** The comedy around Prim is structural (the gap between the form and the reality) not personal. Prim is doing their best with what the system gives them. The WORLD'S BEST mug is funny because the system produced it, not because Prim chose it.

**Why Prim matters structurally:** Prim reads carefully. After the turn, when Tert's reports subtly shift, Prim is the character positioned to notice. The mid-cycle review scene works because Prim's attention is genuine, not bureaucratic. They are paying attention the way a person pays attention, inside a framework that can only express attention as a completed form.

### The New Hire

A junior demon, freshly assigned to the Hauntings department. Still enthusiastic. Still asks questions the protagonist stopped asking years ago. "But why do we do the flickering lights thing? Like, mechanically, what is it supposed to do?" "If the target isn't religious, what does rejecting God even mean?" "Has anyone ever actually seen a soul get harvested?"

The new hire is what the protagonist used to be. This is the contrast that illuminates the protagonist's arc without anyone stating it. The new hire forces the protagonist to either articulate justifications the protagonist no longer believes, or deflect, or sidestep. Every interaction is a small audit of the protagonist's accommodations.

The new hire is not a foil because they are naive. They are a foil because they are earnest. Their earnestness is what the protagonist lost.

### Not in this book

The earlier exploratory sketches of a peer-department Colleague, an Old Timer, and an occupational-hazard Priest are cut from the novella's cast. They are good ideas for other stories in this universe. For this book they would dilute the two-person supporting structure that mirrors the protagonist most sharply.

The occupational-hazard Priest in particular is tempting but structurally wrong for this novella: our target is secular, so a priest appearing is a contrivance the book would have to pay for. Cut cleanly.
