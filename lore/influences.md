# Influences

**Status:** CANONICAL (2026-04-11)

Each entry maps an influence to specific thematic connections and manuscript opportunities. The reading list annotations (backmatter) tell the reader why the work matters. This document tells us, as authors, how to use the influence in the writing.

---

## George Eliot, Middlemarch

**Connection:** Directly in the text. The book Gordon reads.

**What we take:** Eliot's conviction that ordinary life, observed closely enough, contains all the tragedy and beauty of epic literature. Her method: patient, specific, never looking away, never sentimentalizing. This is Tert's method too, though he applies it to haunting rather than narration.

**Manuscript opportunities:**
- The two Middlemarch quotes (ch11 fragment, ch20 full passage) are planted and working.
- Gordon's chapter-reading pace should feel like a real reader's pace through the novel. The bookmark moves. The reader who knows Middlemarch can roughly track where Gordon is.
- Eliot's characters (Dorothea, Casaubon, Lydgate) all pour craft and devotion into projects that don't produce the results they intended. This is Tert. This is Prim. This is Gordon with his garden.

## Albert Camus, The Myth of Sisyphus

**Connection:** The philosophical spine.

**What we take:** The continuation. Not the revolt, not the refusal, not the suicide. The continuation in full knowledge. Sisyphus at the bottom of the hill, watching the boulder roll back down, turning to walk after it. "One must imagine Sisyphus happy" is not ironic. It is the only tenable position.

**Manuscript opportunities:**
- Every character has a boulder. Tert: the craft. Gordon: the reading, the garden. Prim: the forms. Meri: the questions. The book does not rank their boulders.
- The coffee motif ("it is the wrong temperature. I drink it anyway.") is the Sisyphus beat in miniature. The "anyway" is the continuation.
- The ending (Tert opens the next file, pauses, signs it) is Sisyphus turning to walk after the boulder. Not happy, not unhappy. Continuing.

## Dante, Inferno

**Connection:** Hell as organization. The precursor.

**What we take:** Dante gave hell structure. We gave it a break room. The Inferno's precision (this circle for this sin, this punishment for this transgression) becomes our metrics system (Fear Index, Timeline Adherence, Soul Outcome). Dante's contrapasso becomes KPIs. The medieval cosmological order becomes corporate process documentation.

**Manuscript opportunities:**
- The departments table in world.md is the Dante inheritance: each department corresponds roughly to a different mode of horror, the way each circle corresponds to a different sin.
- The theological ambiguity (nobody knows if God is real) is the modern inversion of Dante's certainty. Dante's hell knew exactly why it existed. Our hell has lost the manual.

## C.S. Lewis, The Screwtape Letters

**Connection:** The genre predecessor.

**What we take:** Lewis understood that demons as office workers is funny. Screwtape advises Wormwood on how to corrupt a human soul using bureaucratic language and professional condescension. The distance between Lewis and us: Screwtape believes in the cosmic war. Tert does not know whether there is one. Lewis's satire is Christian apologetics in disguise. Ours has no theology to smuggle in.

**Manuscript opportunities:**
- The Prim/Tert dynamic echoes Screwtape/Wormwood (experienced supervisor, junior operative), but inverted: Prim is not cynical or manipulative. Prim is diligent.
- Lewis's demons are malicious and strategic. Ours are bored and professional. The gap between the two is the gap between the genre's assumption about evil (it is intentional) and the book's argument (it is structural).

## Kazuo Ishiguro, The Remains of the Day

**Connection:** The craft-as-complicity novel.

**What we take:** Stevens's tragedy is that his professional excellence was the vehicle through which someone else's harm was delivered. He cannot separate the quality of his butlering from the politics of the household he butled for. His late-life recognition of this is not dramatic. It is quiet, restrained, expressed through small failures of composure in an otherwise perfectly controlled narrative voice.

**Manuscript opportunities:**
- Tert's post-turn awareness should have the same quality as Stevens's late recognition: not dramatic, not articulated, just a slight change in how the voice holds itself. The irony works slightly harder. The pauses are slightly longer.
- The "eleven minutes" chapter is our equivalent of Stevens's moments of almost-feeling. The professional framework is still intact. Something underneath it is not.
- Stevens never rebels. He continues to buttle. Tert never rebels. He continues to haunt. The continuation is the tragedy, not the awareness.

## John Gardner, Grendel

**Connection:** The monster narrator.

**What we take:** Grendel watches humans from outside. He sees them build meaning through poetry and community. He is intelligent enough to understand what meaning is and isolated enough to be excluded from it. His narration is bitter, funny, philosophical, and ultimately devastating because the interiority is real and the world has no use for it.

**Manuscript opportunities:**
- Tert watching Gordon is Grendel watching the Danes. The monster observes human meaning-making and cannot participate. Gordon reads aloud, tends the garden, sets a table for one. These are acts of meaning. Tert catalogs them as environmental data.
- Grendel's philosophical education (he meets a dragon who tells him the universe is mechanical and meaningless) maps onto the theological ambiguity. The dragon is Management: the voice that says the pipeline works because the pipeline exists.
- Grendel is killed by Beowulf. Tert is not killed. Tert files a report and gets the next assignment. The modern monster's fate is not destruction. It is continuation.

## Hannah Arendt, Eichmann in Jerusalem

**Connection:** The banality of evil.

**What we take:** Arendt's argument is structural, not psychological. Eichmann was not a monster. He was a functionary. The harm was enormous and the person administering it was ordinary. The system did not require malice. It required compliance, and compliance was the path of least resistance.

**Manuscript opportunities:**
- The forms absorb everything. "Delayed due to thermal conditions." "Verbal engagement." "Soul Outcome: indeterminate." The language of the forms is the language of compliance. Tert uses it fluently. The fluency is the complicity.
- Prim reading the forms carefully is the Arendtian counterpoint: diligence within the system is not resistance. It is the system working at a higher resolution.
- The book should never compare demons to Nazis. The structural parallel is enough. The reader who sees it does not need help.

## Herman Melville, Bartleby the Scrivener

**Connection:** The refusal that Tert does not make.

**What we take:** Bartleby prefers not to. He stops copying. He stops eating. He stops living. He does not explain. The narrator (his employer) cannot understand him and cannot save him. Bartleby's refusal is total and it accomplishes nothing except his own death.

**Manuscript opportunities:**
- Tert is the anti-Bartleby. He sees everything Bartleby sees (the meaninglessness of the work, the absurdity of the system) and he does not prefer not to. He prefers not to prefer not to. He circles hat and opens the door.
- The coffee motif is the anti-Bartleby beat: "It is the wrong temperature. I drink it anyway." Bartleby would prefer not to drink it.

## Mary Shelley, Frankenstein

**Connection:** The articulate monster.

**What we take:** The creature can describe what it feels. The description changes nothing. The world responds to what the creature is (a monster), not to what the creature says (a person). Articulation without audience.

**Manuscript opportunities:**
- Tert's narration is the creature's chapters: precise, intelligent, full of observation, and directed at a reader (us) who can hear him but cannot intervene. The system has no use for Tert's interiority. We do.
- Gordon asking "are you all right?" is the one moment in the book where someone responds to Tert as a presence rather than a threat. It is brief. It is not repeated.

## Joan Didion, The Year of Magical Thinking

**Connection:** Grief as precision.

**What we take:** Didion catalogs loss the way a professional catalogs a scene. The shoes. The medical records. The vortex effect (ordinary objects become unbearable). Her method is cold and the coldness is what makes the grief legible. Sentimentality would have insulated the reader. Precision does not.

**Manuscript opportunities:**
- Gordon's house is Didion's house: the grab bar, the evaporated water glass, the expired prescriptions, the imprint in the floor. Every object is a fact. Every fact is grief. The precision is Tert's method and the grief is Gordon's. They produce the same inventory for different reasons.
- Tert reading the prescription labels ("They are not relevant to the work. I read them anyway.") is the Didion impulse: cataloging what does not need to be cataloged, because looking away from it is harder than looking at it.

## David Graeber, Bullshit Jobs

**Connection:** The anthropology of meaningless work.

**What we take:** Graeber argues that many workers know their jobs are meaningless and that this knowledge causes real psychological damage. His taxonomy (flunkies, goons, duct tapers, box tickers, taskmasters) describes organizational roles that exist to justify the organization's existence, not to produce value.

**Manuscript opportunities:**
- The Fear Index, the Soul Outcome projection, the escalation schedule: these are box-ticking. The metrics exist to be measured. The measurements justify the department. The department justifies the metrics. The loop is closed.
- Graeber's observation that people in meaningless jobs often create private meaning through craft (doing the job better than it needs to be done, developing expertise nobody asked for) is Tert exactly. The joist-mapping is not required by the protocol. It is required by Tert.

## Franz Kafka, The Trial / The Castle

**Connection:** Bureaucracy as existential condition.

**What we take:** Kafka's systems are not broken. They are working. The rules are real. The authorities exist. The purposes are never explained. The protagonist navigates the system by trying to understand it, and the system absorbs every attempt at understanding without changing.

**Manuscript opportunities:**
- Prim's Post-it ("please address") is a Kafka beat: an informal communication within a formal system, delivered by an authority who may or may not be exercising discretion, requiring a response whose correct form is ambiguous.
- The transit desk with two doors both marked 1-14B is spatial Kafka: the architecture does not make sense and nobody remarks on this.
- Meri's questions are the protagonist's attempts to understand the system. The system's answer (Operations Manual: "the mechanism is well-established and does not require field-level understanding") is Kafka's answer: the system explains itself by asserting that explanation is unnecessary.
