**Date**: 2026-04-09
**Manuscript**: Demons at Work (13 chapters, ~9,574 words)
**Scope**: Full manuscript, pacing, tension arcs, thematic coherence

## Methodology

Mapped the manuscript's three-movement structure against the canonical outline. Analyzed word-count distribution, chapter pacing, arc completeness, and thematic delivery against the lore's commitments. Checked the turn's positioning and the post-turn section's weight.

## Findings

### HIGH

#### H1. The manuscript is structurally incomplete: post-turn section is critically thin

- **Location**: Chapters 11-13 (1,265 words, 13.2% of manuscript)
- **Problem**: The canonical outline specifies three movements. Movement 1 (setup/execution) should run to ~60%. Movement 3 (completion) should run from ~60% to 100%, meaning ~40% of the manuscript. The manuscript's post-turn section is 13.2%. The outline specifies 18 chapters; the manuscript has 13.

  Missing from the post-turn section:
  1. **Week 4 full escalation**: The outline says "the most technically demanding phase" and "the book's best atmospheric set pieces" should land here. Ch12 provides some post-turn haunting (Mon-Wed of one week) but not the full escalation the outline promises.
  2. **Week 5 resolution and case closure**: Entirely absent. The protocol completion, final shift reports, the transit back to the office.
  3. **Meri's statement scene**: The lore marks Meri's three-stage arc (questions -> evaluating -> statements) as canonical. Stage three does not exist in the manuscript.
  4. **The ending**: The outline specifies: back at the office, close the file, new assignment, the pause, the final image. None of this exists. Ch13 (Prim's mid-cycle review) is the current endpoint.
  5. **Accumulating observations**: The outline says "small observations of the target accumulate. Not scenes. Moments." Post-turn, the manuscript has one Gordon observation (Ch12, Gordon waking to the attic sound). The texture the outline promises is absent.

- **Suggestion**: The manuscript needs approximately 5-8 more chapters to reach the outline's target length (~25,000-30,000 words) and to complete the structural arc. The highest-priority additions are: (a) the ending, (b) Meri's statement scene, (c) at least one full Week 4 horror set piece post-turn.
- **Confidence**: High

#### H2. The turn is positioned at ~87% by word count, not ~60%

- **Location**: Ch10 "Middlemarch" begins at 86.8% of cumulative word count
- **Problem**: The lore says "the turn at roughly the 60% mark." The turn scene begins at 87% of the current manuscript. Even by chapter count (ch10/13 = 77%), it is later than specified.

  This is partly an artifact of H1 (the manuscript is incomplete). If the post-turn section were expanded to its intended length (roughly 10,000-15,000 more words), the turn would shift backward proportionally:
  - At 20,000 total words: turn at 8,309/20,000 = 41.5% (too early)
  - At 25,000 total words: turn at 8,309/25,000 = 33.2% (too early)

  This reveals a structural tension: the pre-turn material is already substantial at 8,309 words. If the total manuscript targets 25,000-30,000 words, the pre-turn section needs to be expanded too, or the turn needs to happen earlier, or the 60% target needs to be revised.

- **Suggestion**: There are two viable approaches:
  1. **Expand both sides**: Add pre-turn content (more Week 2-3 house scenes, more office beats) and post-turn content. Target: turn at roughly 15,000 out of 25,000 words (60%).
  2. **Accept a later turn**: If the pre-turn material is tight as-is and the post-turn material needs ~6,000-8,000 words, accept a turn at ~55% and adjust the lore's "60%" specification.
  
  Approach 2 is more defensible. The pre-turn chapters are efficient and well-paced. Adding padding to hit a percentage target would dilute the manuscript. The post-turn section, however, genuinely needs more weight for the thematic arguments to land (see H1).
- **Confidence**: High (the positioning issue is real; the solution requires authorial judgment)

### MEDIUM

#### M1. Chapter 1 is disproportionately long

- **Location**: Ch1 "On Paper" (1,856 words, 19.4% of manuscript)
- **Problem**: Ch1 is nearly twice as long as the next-longest chapter (Ch3, 1,167 words) and more than seven times the shortest (Ch11, 259 words). The style guide specifies "short chapters" and "clipped, professional" narration. Ch1 does a great deal of work: it establishes the voice, the bureaucracy, the case file contents, the house's acoustic promise, and Tert's professional identity. All of this is good. But the chapter's length relative to the rest creates a pacing asymmetry: the book starts with its longest chapter and then accelerates, which is the opposite of most effective pacing curves.
- **Suggestion**: Consider splitting Ch1 after the Fear Index passage (line 45, "I sign it.") and before the environmental survey (line 47). The first half (bureaucratic absurdity, the pipeline, Tert's professional judgment) and the second half (the house, the photographs, the acoustic revelation) are two distinct movements. Splitting would bring both halves into the 800-1,000 word range, consistent with the rest of the manuscript, and would give the acoustic revelation its own chapter opening (its own weight).
- **Confidence**: Medium (the chapter works as-is, but would benefit from the split at full manuscript length)

#### M2. The "peak" happens before the turn

- **Location**: Ch9 "Wednesday Night" vs outline specification
- **Outline**: "Week 4: Full Escalation. The most technically demanding phase. The demon's best set pieces of the book land here."
- **Manuscript**: The technical peak is in Ch9 (Week 3). "This is atmospheric work at the level I have always known I was capable of and have rarely had a canvas for." The three-part Wednesday night sequence is described as the best work of the assignment.
- **Problem**: The outline says the best set pieces should come after the turn, in Week 4, where "the craft is being performed in full knowledge that it will not work." The manuscript puts the peak before the turn. This means the post-turn haunting (Ch12) is working at a lower intensity than the pre-turn peak, which undercuts the outline's structural intention: that the best work should happen after the realization, not before it.
- **Suggestion**: The Wednesday night sequence is excellent and should stay. But the post-turn chapters need a set piece that matches or exceeds it, executed with the changed awareness. This is the craft-over-outcome arc's payoff: the reader sees that the work got better after the turn, not worse.
- **Confidence**: High

### LOW

#### L1. Office/house alternation is not strictly alternating

- **Location**: Chapters 3-4 (house/house), 6-7 (house/house), 9-10 (house/house)
- **Observation**: Three consecutive house-chapter pairs exist. The outline says the alternation should "feel organic to the demon's actual workday" and should not be "mechanical." The current pattern is organic. The house pairs occur when the haunting demands sustained attention (first visit, early manifestations, the turn). This is correct.
- **Status**: Not a problem. The organic alternation is working.
- **Confidence**: High

## Thematic Arc Verification

Checked the manuscript against the canonical thematic commitments:

### Delivered

1. **"The demon is haunting someone who's already haunted"** (the Kernel): Fully delivered. The grief objects, the absent spouse's presence, Gordon's attribution of all wrongness to grief. The demon's best work is invisible through misattribution. Established in Ch1, paid off throughout.

2. **"One must imagine Sisyphus happy"** (the Philosophical Spine): Delivered for Tert through the craft-over-outcome arc. The reader sees Tert doing meaningless work well and making it into something livable through irony and craft. Gordon's parallel (reading to an empty room) is delivered in Ch10.

3. **"The horror must be real"**: Delivered. The Wednesday night sequence (Ch9) is genuinely atmospheric. The joist-timing technique (Ch4, Ch6) is convincingly detailed. The reader learns how horror works.

4. **"The humor must be dry"**: Delivered throughout. Tert's irony is never performed. The comedy is situational and observational.

5. **"Not a redemption arc"**: Delivered. Ch12: "The work does not change." Tert continues at full capacity. The eleven-minute delay is the only visible change, and even that is explained away in the shift report.

### Partially delivered (due to incompleteness)

6. **"The system absorbs resistance"**: Partially delivered. The eleven minutes becoming "thermal conditions" (Ch12) is one instance. Prim's supplemental thermal survey recommendation (Ch13) is another. But the lore says "Meri's questions get deflected with Management language" and "every act of seeing, questioning, or deviating gets translated back into the system's categories." This needs more post-turn material to fully land.

7. **"Awareness changes the person without changing the system"**: Partially delivered. The turn works. The continuation works. But the manuscript ends too soon for the reader to see Tert carry the awareness forward into the routine. The outline's final image (new case file, the pause) is where this theme would complete.

### Not yet delivered (requires unwritten chapters)

8. **"The final image: the demon reading a new case file"**: Absent.
9. **"Whether awareness without action is meaningful or merely comfortable is the reader's question"**: Cannot be posed without the ending.

## Summary

The manuscript's structure is sound through Ch10 (the turn). The pre-turn material is well-paced, efficiently constructed, and delivers its thematic commitments. The turn itself is the book's best scene. The structural problem is that the manuscript ends at roughly the 55% mark of the intended novella. The post-turn section needs substantial expansion to deliver the craft-over-outcome arc, the system-absorbs-resistance theme, and the ending the outline specifies.
