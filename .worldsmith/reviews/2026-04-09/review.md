# Multi-Agent Editorial Review

**Date**: 2026-04-09
**Manuscript**: Demons at Work (13 chapters, ~9,574 words)
**Work**: Demons at Work (novella, single-work project)
**Recommendation**: needs-revision

## Executive Summary

The manuscript is an exceptional first-half draft. Tert's voice is fully realized, the dual register (comedy and pathos) operates exactly as designed, and the turn scene (Ch10, "Middlemarch") is devastating in its restraint. The prose is mechanically clean and the thematic architecture is being delivered with precision through the pre-turn material. The manuscript's problem is not quality; it is completeness. The post-turn section (Ch11-13, 1,265 words, 13.2% of the manuscript) is critically thin against the outline's specification of roughly 40% of the novella. The ending, Meri's statement scene, Week 4-5 content, and the final image do not yet exist. The book as written is a strong ~10,000-word first movement with an excellent turn, followed by a sketch of the third movement. What exists is publication-quality prose; what is missing is roughly half the novella.

**Strengths:**
1. Tert's narrative voice is flawless: dry, self-aware, professional, never winking (voice-auditor)
2. The dual register works at every level, from the macro (comedy in office, pathos in house) to the sentence level (the coffee motif, the margin-pad notes) (craft-auditor, voice-auditor)
3. The Middlemarch plant-and-payoff across Ch1/Ch3/Ch10 is expert-level setup (craft-auditor)
4. The horror craft descriptions are genuinely instructive and atmospheric (craft-auditor)
5. Ch7 "The Garden" (324 words) is a perfect miniature that demonstrates the book's full range in compressed form (craft-auditor)
6. The "I circle hat" beat is instant world/character establishment (voice-auditor)
7. Anti-cliche rules are honored throughout; no em-dashes, no fourth-wall breaks, no redemption signals (voice-auditor, consistency-auditor)

**Key Issues:**
1. The manuscript is structurally incomplete: post-turn section needs ~10,000-15,000 more words (structure-auditor)
2. The technical peak occurs before the turn (Ch9), contradicting the outline's specification that the best set pieces come after (structure-auditor)
3. Meri's canonical three-stage arc is missing its final beat (consistency-auditor, structure-auditor)
4. "Which" clause density in Ch5 exceeds the style guide's "short punches" principle (craft-auditor)

**Finding Counts**: HIGH: 2 | MEDIUM: 6 | LOW: 5

## HIGH Issues

### H1. Post-turn section is critically thin (source: structure-auditor)
- **Location**: Chapters 11-13 (1,265 words, 13.2% of manuscript)
- **Quoted text**: The manuscript ends with Ch13: "Prim noticed the pattern and asked the right question in the wrong language. The form gave them the only answer it had."
- **Problem**: The canonical outline specifies Movement 3 (post-turn completion) at roughly 40% of the novella. The manuscript delivers 13.2%. Missing: Week 4 full escalation, Week 5 resolution, Meri's statement scene, the case closure, the next assignment, and the final image ("the demon reading a new case file with the same professional attention... before the book started"). Five of the outline's eighteen planned chapters are absent.
- **Suggestion**: Write chapters covering: (a) at least one full Week 4 horror set piece executed with post-turn awareness, (b) Meri's statement, (c) Week 5 completion protocol, (d) case closure and final report, (e) the next assignment / final image. Target 6,000-10,000 additional words to bring the post-turn section to 35-40% of the finished novella.
- **Cross-verified**: Confirmed by consistency-auditor (Meri's arc incomplete) and by direct manuscript verification (Ch13 is the final chapter; no ending exists).

### H2. Turn positioned at ~87% of current manuscript, not ~60% (source: structure-auditor)
- **Location**: Ch10 "Middlemarch" begins at word 8,309 of 9,574 (86.8%)
- **Quoted text**: The lore specifies "the turn at roughly the 60% mark."
- **Problem**: The turn is far later than specified. This is partly because the post-turn section is thin (H1), but even with full expansion, the pre-turn material (8,309 words) would put the turn at 33% of a 25,000-word novella or 28% of a 30,000-word one. The pre-turn section may need expansion too, or the 60% target needs revision.
- **Suggestion**: The pre-turn material is tight and efficient; do not pad it. Instead: (a) accept a turn closer to 45-55% rather than 60%, (b) expand the post-turn section substantially (see H1), and (c) update the lore's percentage estimate. A turn at 50% with a substantial post-turn completion section would be structurally sound for a novella of this type.
- **Cross-verified**: Confirmed by word count analysis. The pre-turn chapters are individually well-paced (no filler identified by craft-auditor), so the issue is not bloat but rather that the post-turn section has not yet been written.

## MEDIUM Issues

### M1. Technical peak occurs before the turn (source: structure-auditor)
- **Location**: Ch9 "Wednesday Night" (Week 3) vs outline's specification for Week 4
- **Quoted text**: Ch9: "This is atmospheric work at the level I have always known I was capable of and have rarely had a canvas for."
- **Problem**: The outline says Week 4 should contain "the most technically demanding phase" and "the demon's best set pieces," executed "in full knowledge of futility." The manuscript puts the peak in Week 3, before the turn. Post-turn haunting (Ch12) operates at lower intensity.
- **Suggestion**: The Wednesday night sequence is excellent and should stay in Week 3 as a strong pre-turn peak. But the post-turn chapters need a set piece that equals or surpasses it, showing that the craft got better after the awareness, not worse. This is the thematic payoff: the best work of Tert's career happens after he knows it will not matter.
- **Cross-verified**: Confirmed against outline. The outline explicitly states the best set pieces come in Week 4, post-turn.

### M2. "Which" clause accumulation in Ch5 (source: craft-auditor)
- **Location**: Ch5 "Good Acoustics" (672 words, 6 "which" clauses)
- **Quoted text**: Line 10: "Prim stops by my desk on Tuesday morning, which means either there is a scheduling conflict or they have read one of my shift reports. Both would be unusual. Prim is carrying a clipboard, which they always are, and a mug that says WORLD'S BEST and nothing else, which was issued to all middle management during a morale initiative whose purpose and origin nobody remembers."
- **Problem**: Three "which" clauses in a single sentence. The density in this chapter (1 per 112 words) is more than double the manuscript average. The style guide specifies "short punches."
- **Suggestion**: Break one or two clauses into separate sentences. Example: "Prim is carrying a clipboard. They always are. The mug says WORLD'S BEST and nothing else. It was issued during a morale initiative whose purpose and origin nobody remembers."
- **Cross-verified**: Confirmed by count. Pattern is consistent with Tert's analytical voice but overdense in this chapter.

### M3. Meri's canonical arc incomplete (source: consistency-auditor, structure-auditor)
- **Location**: Across manuscript; lore specification in characters.md and themes.md
- **Quoted text**: Characters.md (resolved): "Meri progresses from collecting data (writing in margins) to evaluating answers (stops writing) to making a statement (final scene)."
- **Problem**: Stages 1-2 are beautifully executed. Stage 3 (the statement) does not exist. The lore marks this as a resolved canonical decision.
- **Suggestion**: Write a post-turn office scene where Meri makes a statement rather than asking a question. The themes doc's open question #3 asks what Meri says. This scene belongs in the post-turn section among the unwritten chapters.
- **Cross-verified**: Confirmed by both specialists and by manuscript search. Meri's last substantive appearance is Ch8; Ch11 notes her absence.

### M4. Grab bar height inconsistency (source: consistency-auditor)
- **Location**: Ch1 line 67 ("about waist height") vs Ch3 line 29 ("hip height")
- **Quoted text**: Ch1: "a grab bar installed on the left-hand wall at about waist height, bolted into a stud." Ch3: "The grab bar is on the left wall at hip height, bolted into a stud."
- **Problem**: Same object described at different heights by the same observer.
- **Suggestion**: Harmonize to "hip height" (accurate for ADA bars). The Ch1 description is from a photograph; the Ch3 description is in person. If the discrepancy is intentional (Tert correcting his photo estimate), make this explicit.
- **Cross-verified**: Confirmed by text search.

### M5. Prim's dialogue lacks distinctiveness (source: voice-auditor)
- **Location**: Ch5 and Ch13
- **Quoted text**: "That is good field judgment." / "The numbers look strong." / "The work looks solid, Tert. Keep it up."
- **Problem**: Prim's lines read as generic corporate management, not as the specific person the lore describes (someone who values process the way a legal professional values process). The lore says Prim's attention is "genuine, not bureaucratic," but the dialogue sounds bureaucratic. The genuineness comes through only in Tert's narration about Prim, not in Prim's own words.
- **Suggestion**: Give Prim one or two speech patterns that distinguish process-care from generic management. Legal-professional precision: conditional phrasing, careful hedging, documented-rather-than-noted language.
- **Cross-verified**: Craft-auditor concurs that the Ch13 closing line about Prim ("asked the right question in the wrong language") does the thematic work beautifully, but it is Tert's analysis, not Prim's voice.

### M6. "Not X" corrective fragment pattern density (source: craft-auditor)
- **Location**: Throughout (15 occurrences in 9,574 words)
- **Quoted text**: Selected: "Not the scaring. The calibration." / "Not wake. Shift." / "Not fear. Not dread."
- **Problem**: A strong voice signature that may become mechanical at current density (1 per 638 words overall, but 1 per 142 words in Ch6). The best instances are brilliant ("Not wake. Shift."). The weaker ones are doing clarification rather than characterization.
- **Suggestion**: Reduce by 2-3 instances in the densest chapters (Ch3 and Ch6). Keep the strongest uses. This is a calibration issue, not a pattern to eliminate.
- **Cross-verified**: Voice-auditor agrees this is a voice feature, not a defect, but concurs that Ch6's density is high.

## LOW Issues

### L1. Basement entrance: lore vs manuscript (source: consistency-auditor)
- **Location**: Ch1 line 49 vs world.md
- **Problem**: Lore describes "a basement with its own separate staircase and a door that does not quite hang right." Manuscript describes "a basement with an exterior entrance." Different architectural features.
- **Suggestion**: Update lore to match manuscript. The exterior entrance is the operational detail that appears in working chapters.

### L2. Recliner vs armchairs (source: consistency-auditor)
- **Location**: world.md vs Ch3
- **Problem**: Lore specifies a recliner; manuscript uses paired armchairs. The armchairs are dramatically superior (facing configuration, the imprint, Tert sitting in the wife's chair).
- **Suggestion**: Update lore to match manuscript.

### L3. "I do not" negative construction frequency (source: craft-auditor)
- **Location**: Throughout (20 occurrences)
- **Problem**: None. This is Tert's formal, un-contracted register. Frequency is appropriate for the voice.
- **Suggestion**: Monitor but do not reduce.

### L4. Mid-cycle review timing label (source: consistency-auditor)
- **Location**: Ch13
- **Problem**: "Mid-Cycle" review covers weeks 1-4 of a 5-week cycle, making it a late-cycle review. Possibly intentional bureaucratic humor.
- **Suggestion**: If intentional, consider one line of Tert acknowledging the naming. If unintentional, rename.

### L5. Chapter 1 length relative to other chapters (source: structure-auditor)
- **Location**: Ch1 (1,856 words vs manuscript average of 736)
- **Problem**: Ch1 is 2.5x the average chapter length. Does important work but creates a pacing asymmetry. At full novella length this will be less pronounced.
- **Suggestion**: Consider splitting after the Fear Index passage (line 45) when the manuscript is closer to complete. The acoustic revelation deserves its own opening.
