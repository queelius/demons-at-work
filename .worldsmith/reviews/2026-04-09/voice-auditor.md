# Voice Auditor Report

**Date**: 2026-04-09
**Manuscript**: Demons at Work (13 chapters, ~9,574 words)
**Scope**: Full manuscript, character voice consistency and dialogue distinctiveness

## Methodology

Evaluated Tert's narrative voice against the style guide's specifications (dry, professional, ironic, first person present tense, fully self-aware). Assessed dialogue distinctiveness for Prim and Meri. Checked POV discipline and register consistency.

## Findings

### HIGH

*No HIGH voice issues found.*

### MEDIUM

#### M1. Prim's dialogue could be more distinctive

- **Location**: Ch5 and Ch13
- **Ch5 examples**:
  - "I wanted to touch base on forty-seven-C."
  - "How are you finding the environmental parameters?"
  - "That is good field judgment."
  - "Keep me posted."
  - "I will have more data by week two"
- **Ch13 examples**:
  - "The numbers look strong."
  - "The work looks solid, Tert. Keep it up."
  - "Is there an environmental factor I should flag for Intake?"
- **Problem**: Prim's dialogue is competent corporate-speak, but it reads as generic management language rather than a specific person's voice. The lore specifies Prim as someone who "values process the way a legal professional values process" and whose attention is "genuine, not bureaucratic." The manuscript's Prim sounds bureaucratic. The genuineness underneath is not audible in the dialogue itself; it comes through only in Tert's narration about Prim (especially the excellent final line of Ch13: "Prim noticed the pattern and asked the right question in the wrong language").
- **Suggestion**: Give Prim one or two speech patterns that distinguish process-care from generic management. A legal professional's language is precise in a different way from a corporate manager's. Prim might say "documented" instead of "noted," or use conditional phrasing ("If the thermal profile is affecting layering, we should flag it now rather than at closure") that reveals careful thinking inside the bureaucratic frame. The goal: a reader should be able to identify Prim's lines without dialogue tags.
- **Confidence**: Medium

### LOW

#### L1. Tert's voice is remarkably consistent

- **Location**: All 13 chapters
- **Observation**: The first-person present tense voice is maintained flawlessly. Tert never breaks register. The dry, professional, analytical tone holds across office scenes and house scenes. The shift between registers (comedy in office, restraint in house) happens through what Tert notices, not through how Tert speaks, exactly as the style guide specifies. This is the book's most difficult technical achievement and it is fully executed.
- **Specific strengths**:
  - Tert's self-awareness is never performed for the reader. He does not wink. He observes his own behavior with the same precision he applies to everything else. Ch2: "which is the phrase Management uses when they do not want to answer a question. I have picked it up the way people pick up phrases from the organization they work in, without deciding to and without being proud of it." This is self-awareness rendered as observation, not confession.
  - The shift from "the subject" to "Gordon" (Ch6, line 22) is a voice-level event that registers as a structural shift. Tert names it ("which the Operations Manual advises against") and immediately undercuts any sentimentality ("The Operations Manual has not spent ten days in his house"). The undercut is Tert's irony protecting him, and the reader can see both the protection and the thing being protected.
  - Post-turn, the voice does not change. This is correct and difficult. Ch12's opening paragraph insists: "The work does not change. I want to be clear about this." The insistence is new. The need to be clear about it is new. The voice itself is the same. This is the right move.
- **Status**: No action needed. This is a strength.
- **Confidence**: High

#### L2. Meri's voice progression is effective but limited by screen time

- **Location**: Ch2, Ch5, Ch8
- **Observation**: Meri's three appearances show clear progression. Ch2: asks questions, writes answers in margins (collecting). Ch5: reports what the manual says, pushes back on the non-answer ("That is not an answer"). Ch8: asks a deeper question that reaches the craft level, does not write the answer down (evaluating). Each appearance is brief and distinct. Meri's earnestness is conveyed through directness: short sentences, no hedging, no corporate language. This is the correct contrast with both Tert (ironic, appositional) and Prim (bureaucratic).
- **Problem**: Meri has roughly 150 words of dialogue in the entire manuscript. The progression works, but the third stage (statement) is absent. This is a completeness issue, not a voice issue.
- **Status**: Voice is working. More screen time needed for the arc's final beat.
- **Confidence**: High

#### L3. Gordon has no dialogue

- **Location**: Throughout
- **Observation**: Gordon is seen entirely through Tert's observation. He never speaks a word of dialogue. The only audible Gordon is "reads aloud" (Ch10, described but not quoted) and "laughs" (Ch9, described). This is consistent with the lore ("seen entirely through the demon's eyes") and the POV constraint. The reader builds Gordon from Tert's observations of his actions, his objects, his routines. This works. It also means the reader never hears Gordon's voice, which could be a limitation if the novella needs the reader to feel Gordon's interiority more strongly in later chapters.
- **Suggestion**: The reading-aloud scene in Ch10 might benefit from one quoted sentence of Middlemarch in Gordon's voice. Not Tert's summary of what it sounded like, but the actual words filling the room. This would break the POV filter for exactly one beat, which is either wrong or exactly right. Flagging for the author's judgment.
- **Confidence**: Low (this is a creative choice, not an error)

## Anti-Cliche Compliance

Checked against the anti-cliche rules in style.md:

- "The demon never describes themselves as evil." **Compliant.** Tert never uses the word "evil."
- "No mwahaha energy." **Compliant.** Tert's professional tone precludes villainy.
- "Hell is not edgy. It's bureaucratic." **Compliant.** The office is fluorescent, clipboard-bound, and exactly the right temperature of disappointing.
- "Humans are not stupid." **Compliant.** Gordon is pragmatic, attentive, and dignified. He finds the thermostat.
- "No breaking the fourth wall." **Compliant.** Tert never addresses the reader.
- "Not Pratchett, Adams, or Palahniuk." **Compliant.** The voice is drier and more internal than any of these. Closest comparison: a Coen Brothers narrator, as the style guide suggests.

## Summary

The voice is the manuscript's greatest achievement. Tert's narrative persona is fully realized, consistent, and distinctive. The dual register operates through observation, not tonal gear-shifting. The anti-cliche rules are honored throughout. The only actionable finding is Prim's dialogue, which could benefit from more distinctive speech patterns to match the character's lore-specified depth.
